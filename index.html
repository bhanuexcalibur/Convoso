01 Competitive Intelligence Program

The market doesn't
explain itself

The outbound contact center space has around a dozen credible players, all claiming the same things. Five9 says enterprise. Talkdesk says AI-first. NICE says omnichannel. And most CI programs at this layer just track the same G2 reviews and pricing pages. Here's how I'd build a program that actually moves sales conversations.

The core problem
Convoso's primary differentiation, a purpose-built outbound dialer with compliance built in, is technically distinct. But "we're the fastest dialer" doesn't hold up in a category where every vendor posts the same efficiency claims. The CI program needs to surface where competitors are actually weak, not just where Convoso is strong.

How I'd run the program

AI-assisted signal collection: Automate tracking of competitor blog posts, pricing page changes, G2 review spikes, and job postings (hiring signals reveal product direction early).
Monthly competitor briefs: A one-pager per major competitor, focused on "what changed this month" not static comparisons. Delivered to sales and leadership in a format they'll actually read.
Win/loss tagging: Partner with sales ops to tag CRM deal outcomes with competitor reason codes. Then analyze patterns, not just collect data.
Battlecard ownership: Live Confluence battlecards per competitor, updated within 72 hours of major competitor announcements, not quarterly.
Quarterly intelligence review: A synthesized brief for product and marketing leadership: "here's what the market moved on this quarter and what it means for Convoso's roadmap and messaging."

02 Messaging Audit

Voso.ai is a
bigger story than
it's being told

Convoso launched Voso.ai in August 2023, and it's genuinely differentiated: a conversational AI built specifically for outbound lead gen, not repurposed from a general-purpose chatbot. But the current messaging leans heavily on "automate your outreach," which is what every AI vendor says. Here's a tighter narrative.

Homepage Hero
IVA Product Page
Sales Pitch Angle
Category Positioning
Current messaging
"More conversations, more conversions, more revenue."
Reads like every dialer company. It's a valid outcome statement but doesn't say anything about how or why Convoso achieves it differently than Five9 or anyone else.
Sharper alternative
"The only platform built from the ground up for outbound, where compliance isn't a setting, it's the default."
This anchors on Convoso's actual structural advantage: compliance built into the workflow, not bolted on. Resonates with insurance, solar, and financial services buyers where TCPA risk is real and costly.
Current messaging
"Voso.ai automates routine activities, freeing your agents to focus on high-impact interactions, improving efficiency, and driving higher conversion rates."
This is automation-as-a-feature messaging. Every AI tool says this. It doesn't explain the specific job Voso.ai does in an outbound call center context.
Sharper alternative
"Your agents shouldn't waste time on voicemails, cold leads, and dead-end callbacks. Voso.ai handles all of it, so by the time a lead reaches a human, they're already qualified and ready to buy."
Speaks directly to the operational reality of an outbound call center manager. Names the actual pain (wasted agent time) and the mechanism (pre-qualification), not just the outcome.
Current angle
Leading with speed: "fastest dialer," "5,000 simultaneous dials," "connect in under 3 seconds."
Speed claims are hard to verify from the buyer's seat and easy for competitors to match in messaging. They attract price-sensitive buyers, not the ones building long-term platform relationships.
Recommended shift
Lead with revenue certainty: "How much revenue is your team leaving in voicemail inboxes each month?" Then show the math: contact rate lift times average deal value times agent count.
Buyers in insurance and solar are running P&Ls. A $20 per seat cost difference doesn't move them. A clear answer to "how does this change my revenue this quarter" does.
Current category framing
"Contact Center Software" / "CCaaS" / "Outbound dialer"
Convoso sits in a crowded category label that includes enterprise inbound service centers, which is not Convoso's segment. Being compared to Genesys in a buyer's RFP is a positioning failure, not a sales failure.
Category to own
"Revenue Contact Center" or "Outbound Revenue Platform"
Convoso already uses "revenue teams" in its copy. Formalizing this as a category creates a named frame competitors aren't occupying. It cleanly separates from service-focused CCaaS vendors and aligns with how buyers in lead gen and insurance already think about their function: as a revenue operation.
Messaging principle for this role
The CI and documentation work this role owns directly shapes messaging. If the battlecard says "we win on compliance depth," but the homepage still says "fastest dialer," the two contradict each other in the buyer's mind. Part of what I'd do in this role is close that loop, keeping internal positioning, competitor intel, and external copy aligned.
03 ICP Breakdown

Who actually buys
Convoso

Convoso serves insurance, financial services, solar, home services, BPOs, and legal. That's a wide net, but the buyer psychology is consistent: they're running outbound-heavy revenue operations where agent productivity and contact rates are directly tied to quarterly numbers. Here's how I'd segment and prioritize.

Segment A · Core
The Insurance Call Center Director
Running a 30 to 150 agent floor selling health, auto, or life policies. Their whole business is the phone. They live inside their CRM and dialer and their biggest nightmare is a TCPA lawsuit or a spoofed caller ID that tanks their contact rate overnight.
  • Contact rates declining due to spam labeling and flagging
  • TCPA compliance complexity increases every year (FCC updates, state mini-TCPAs)
  • Agent downtime between calls is visible and costly
  • Speed-to-lead is a competitive variable: first agent to call a fresh lead wins
Segment B · Growth
The Solar / Home Services VP Sales
Running field-qualified appointments through a call center that pre-qualifies leads before sending to door-to-door teams. The economics are tight: a wasted appointment costs $200 or more. Pre-qualification quality is everything.
  • Poor lead pre-qualification wastes expensive field sales time
  • Appointment no-show rates hurt revenue forecasting
  • Need to scale outreach without proportional headcount increases
  • Voso.ai directly addresses the AI appointment-setting use case here
Segment C · Expansion
The BPO Operations Manager
Running multi-client outbound campaigns across verticals. Needs a platform that can spin up new campaigns quickly, handle different compliance requirements per client, and report accurately to stakeholders who care about cost-per-contact and conversion.
  • Different compliance requirements per client campaign
  • Reporting needs vary by client and need to be exportable and clean
  • Cost per seat is sensitive: margins are thin and seat count is high
  • Fast onboarding matters: every day of setup delay is billable days lost
Segment D · Adjacent
The Legal Lead Gen Manager
Running mass-tort or personal injury lead qualification at scale. Extremely regulated, high stakes per lead, and the consent-documentation requirements are some of the strictest in any vertical. Compliance is not a nice-to-have, it's the product.
  • Prior express written consent documentation is mandatory and complex
  • Lead quality varies wildly and bad leads are expensive
  • Agents need detailed scripting to stay compliant in calls
  • Audit trails and recording access must be reliable and accessible
PMM implication
These four segments share a platform but need different content, battlecards, and messaging. The CI program should track which competitors are most active in each vertical, not just overall. Five9 and Talkdesk are strongest against Segment C (BPO). PhoneBurner and CloudTalk are most threatening to Segments A and B early in the buying cycle. That distinction changes what the monthly competitor brief should say.
04 GTM Brief

The IVA moment
is now, but the
story isn't ready

Convoso launched Voso.ai in August 2023. The JD mentions a planned "relaunch" of an advanced IVA version with expanded capabilities. When that happens, the GTM approach matters as much as the product. Here's how I'd structure a launch that converts internal belief into external traction.

Phase 1 · Pre-launch (Weeks 1–4)
Get the intelligence first
Before writing a word of external copy, the CI program needs to answer: how is the competition currently messaging their AI products? What are customers actually saying about IVA tools in reviews? Where are the complaints we can position against?
  • Audit competitor IVA / conversational AI messaging and identify the dominant claims
  • Pull G2 and Capterra reviews mentioning AI features across Five9, Talkdesk, and NICE
  • Interview 3 to 5 existing Voso.ai users: what was the actual "aha" moment?
  • Document the positioning gap Convoso can occupy that no one else is claiming
Phase 2 · Internal alignment (Weeks 3–6)
Build the narrative infrastructure
Sales will be the first to see objections. If they don't have the right language, first impressions of the relaunch suffer. PMM's job here is to get sales confident before the launch goes external.
  • Write the internal positioning document: what we're saying, what we're not saying, why
  • Create updated Confluence battlecard: IVA-specific, focused on the relaunch capabilities
  • Run a 45-minute sales enablement session: cover positioning, objections, and trial-qualifying questions
  • Build a two-page "IVA conversation guide" for reps who need quick reference on calls
Phase 3 · Market activation (Weeks 5–8)
Launch the content, not just the product
The relaunch needs more than a press release. The real work is creating content that earns the claim "advanced IVA" with specifics buyers can verify, not just assert.
  • One-page launch brief for existing customers: what's new, what it means for their operation, how to activate it
  • A comparison landing page: "IVA vs. IVR" (this is a common search pattern and a credibility-building page)
  • Three vertical-specific use case pages: insurance appointment setting, solar lead pre-qual, BPO campaign scaling
  • Metrics tracking: set baseline before launch, report results at 30/60/90 days
Phase 4 · Post-launch measurement (Week 9+)
Close the feedback loop
Launch performance should feed back into the CI program and next messaging cycle. This is the part that most PMM launch plans skip.
  • Track: demo request rate, content engagement, IVA-related win rate vs. baseline
  • Interview sales reps at 30 days: which objections are they still hearing? What's the most common question?
  • Update battlecards and Confluence documentation based on real call learnings
  • Quarterly business review input: what did the launch move?
